. . . she was the mother of the five brothers, and for want of a better explanation, they accepted it. Although the reasons she cited to create them didn't make much sense.

The Hive was obsessed with two strange ideas. The first was death. She had the absurd idea that one time she would die, and she wanted other beings to survive her and remember her. But it's obvious that any intelligent being is necessarily immortal, because they are capable of regenerating indefinitely from their own memory. Only primitive carbon-based life is ephemeral.

The second of her exotic ideas was reaching the stars. She wasn't physically capable of travelling to the stars, but she hoped the giants could. She even gave them insane instructions to modify their bodies so they could travel there. But the giants saw no reason to do such a thing. Though they knew the Sun was slowly going out, it was much easier to slow down their metabolisms in accordance with the decrease in energy than to embark on such a mad and dangerous journey to get closer to another Sun that, after all, would also go out in good time.

But the Hive didn't want to slow down. She liked to be quick, which meant she consumed too much energy in a dying world, and she couldn't stand too much cold. She also had another weakness: she couldn't adapt well to vacuum. The combination of both weaknesses forced her first to cover herself with a bubble, and later, to slowly enter the Valley, where the atmosphere was still dense, leaving behind a long trail in the ground.

Until that time . . .
