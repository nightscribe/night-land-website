. . . . . carefully examined his surroundings, in search of sources of energy for the long journey. But the landscape was definitely not promising.
 
He was in the middle of an immense plain, dark, silent, still. The giant was used to feeling the Earth move under his feet, waving, writhing and tearing under the forces of the inner fires, but on this occasion, he didn't feel the slightest vibration.
 
The pale Sun of the times of his childhood had gone out almost completely, and it wasn't visible any more.
 
His eyes, delicate sensors that could perceive a flame as small as a candle's on the edge of the horizon, tried hard to find the tiniest of those bright crevices that warn about the imminent appearance of a lake of lava, or the smallest marsh fire.
 
Nothing.
 
He'd be in the middle of the most absolute blackness if it weren't for the stars in the sky, and the stars were so dim...
 
Even though the Sun didn't cloud their light any more, they didn't look any brighter. They too were dying away. Even as he looked at them, revolving in their fast dance around the celestial North Pole, some of them were visibly fading.
 
Or perhaps they were moving away from the Earth.
 
He wondered if the Hive had wanted so much to travel to the stars because she knew they were all going to run out of reach.
 
The silence, too, was almost total. Apart from the deep bass of the distant Earth-current, the faint screech of the Sun, not yet totally dead, and the much fainter whistling of the stars, nothing.
 
If it wasn't for the Earth-current and the call from his people, he could have believed that the Earth, just like the Sun, had exhausted all its energy.
 
The giant wasn't sure . . .
